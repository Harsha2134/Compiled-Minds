Solution Overview
  • Our solution is a 4-layer, multi-signal candidate ranking pipeline that processes 1,00,000 candidates against a Job Description using semantic NLP (SBERT embeddings), rule-based fraud detection, and weighted ensemble scoring — producing a ranked output with explainable per-candidate justifications.
  • Unlike traditional keyword-matching ATS systems, our approach uses sentence-level semantic similarity via SBERT (capturing synonyms, adjacent skills, and context), a dedicated fraud & anomaly detection layer to penalise suspicious profiles, and a transparent weighted scoring formula combining semantic fit, skill match, experience, education, and quality signals — going far beyond simple keyword overlap.

JD Understanding & Candidate Evaluation
  • Key requirements extracted from the JD include: required skills (programming languages, frameworks, tools), minimum experience threshold, education level, location preference, and role-specific context. Our parser decomposes each JD field into weighted requirement vectors used for downstream scoring.
  • The most important candidate signals are: semantic skill alignment (cosine similarity of SBERT embeddings), years of experience vs. JD threshold, education tier, profile completeness, and fraud-risk penalty. Our solution evaluates fit by embedding the full candidate profile alongside the full JD text using SBERT (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) — capturing synonyms and transferable skills that keyword matching entirely misses.

Ranking Methodology
  • Candidates are streamed from a 1,00,000-entry JSONL pool, scored via our 4-layer pipeline, and ranked in descending order by composite score. Scores are normalised to [0, 1] and ties broken deterministically by candidate_id for strict reproducibility.
  • Models & algorithms used: SBERT all-MiniLM-L6-v2 for semantic embeddings, cosine similarity for JD-candidate matching, rule-based heuristics for experience & education scoring, and anomaly detection rules (duplicate contacts, impossible date timelines, keyword stuffing) for fraud penalties.
  • Multiple candidate signals combined via configurable weighted ensemble: Semantic Similarity (40%) + Skill Match (25%) + Experience Score (20%) + Education Score (10%) + Quality Bonus (5%) — with fraud penalty deducted before final normalisation.

Explainability & Data Validation
  • Ranking decisions are explained through a per-candidate justification string that enumerates the top contributing signals (e.g. "Semantic similarity: 0.87 · Skill match: Python/FastAPI found · Experience: 5 yrs meets 4 yr threshold") — making every decision auditable and human-readable.
  • Hallucinations and unsupported justifications are prevented by generating explanations only from extracted candidate data fields — no LLM is used for justification generation, so all claims are directly traceable to parsed profile data with zero fabrication risk.
  • Inconsistent, low-quality, or suspicious profiles are handled by our L2 Fraud & Anomaly Detection layer: shared phone/email across candidates, implausible overlapping employment tenures, inflated generic titles, or missing critical fields each trigger a score penalty multiplier — keeping fraudulent profiles ranked below genuine high-quality candidates.

End-to-End Workflow
  • JD Input → Parse → Decompose Job Description into structured requirement vectors (skills, experience threshold, education, location, role context).

    → Candidate Streaming → Stream 1,00,000 candidates from JSONL using memory-efficient generators.

    → L2 Fraud Detection → Flag and penalise suspicious profiles (duplicate contacts, impossible timelines, keyword stuffing).

    → L3 Semantic Scoring → SBERT batch-encode JD + candidate profiles; compute cosine similarity + skill/experience/education sub-scores.

    → L4 Ensemble & Rank → Weighted composite aggregation + fraud penalty → normalise → sort → output ranked submission CSV with justifications.

System Architecture
  • Layer 1 – Data Ingestion & Parsing: Generator-based JSONL streaming (never loads full 1,00,000 candidates into RAM), JD field decomposition, text normalisation, structured candidate object construction.
  • Layer 2 – Fraud & Anomaly Detection: Rule-based checks for duplicate contacts, impossible date overlaps, keyword stuffing, and completeness signals. Outputs a fraud_score penalty per candidate applied at the final aggregation stage.
  • Layer 3 – Semantic Scoring Engine: Batch SBERT encoding (batch_size=256) of JD + candidate profiles, cosine similarity computation, plus rule-based sub-scores for skill match, experience alignment, and education tier.
  • Layer 4 – Ensemble & Output: Weighted composite aggregation, fraud penalty deduction, [0,1] normalisation, deterministic tie-breaking by candidate_id, and final ranked CSV output with per-candidate justification strings.

Results & Performance
  • Submission Validation: compiled_minds.csv passed the official validator with zero format errors — exactly 100 rows, correct headers (rank, candidate_id, score), strictly non-increasing scores, and valid deterministic tie-breaking.
  • Ranking Quality: Top-100 candidates scored between 0.71–0.94 composite score; the bottom of the pool averaged 0.18 — demonstrating strong signal discrimination between highly relevant and irrelevant profiles.
  • Runtime Performance: Full 1,00,000-candidate pipeline completes in under 12 minutes on a standard CPU laptop using batch encoding + caching — no GPU required, meeting the challenge's compute constraints.
  • Fraud Coverage: The anomaly layer flagged ~3.2% of candidates with suspicious signals (duplicate contacts, impossible date overlaps, keyword stuffing), preventing fraudulent profiles from occupying top-ranked positions.
